# Biome Profiles — Authoring Source of Truth

Reference document for all three biomes and the three Fringe zones. Use alongside `SPECIES_PROFILES.md` when writing observation pools, event text, and biome-specific copy. Cross-check against `src/data/species.js` for biome comfort values.

**Structure of each profile:**
- **Physical environment** — what the researcher experiences directly
- **Sensory character** — light, sound, smell, temperature, what the body notices
- **Vertical structure** — layers and zones within the biome
- **Time character** — how fast things change, what a cycle feels like here
- **Residents** — which species live here and how they use the space
- **What healthy looks like** — the baseline the researcher calibrates to
- **What stressed looks like** — the signals that something is wrong
- **Event character** — what kinds of events originate here
- **Researcher arc** — how familiarity changes the researcher's relationship to this biome
- **Distinctive character** — the one quality that defines it against the others
- **Writing rules** — tone, emphasis, what to avoid

---

## HIGHGROWTH

*Resident species: Feltmoss, Vellin, Keth, Skethran (primary), Mordath (home biome)*
*Biome comfort ≥ 0.6: Vellin (0.6 in Understory), Skethran (0.8 in Understory), Mordath (0.8/0.4)*

### Physical environment

Dense vertical growth from near-ground level to an open upper zone where Keth soars. The structural elements — call them column-growths, the researcher has no better term — rise from the biome floor and branch irregularly in the upper third, creating a canopy surface covered almost entirely in Feltmoss. The column-growths themselves are bare except at their upper surfaces; all horizontal area is colonized by Feltmoss in good conditions. Below the canopy proper is the mid-canopy: a tangled zone of lateral growth and climbing routes where Vellin spend most of their time. At the very base, the canopy floor is shaded and cooler — this is where Skethran hunts and where Vellin compress under predator pressure.

The structural elements are not trees. The researcher stopped trying to classify them early on. They are load-bearing and phototropic but the similarity to any familiar structure ends there. They do not have bark. The surface is smooth and slightly waxy, which makes them poor footing for anything without specialised locomotion — which is why the canopy is effectively Vellin territory.

### Sensory character

**Light:** Abundant through the upper zone; filtered and interrupted in the mid-canopy; dim at the canopy floor. The light source is not described — it comes from above, it supports photosynthesis (or its alien equivalent), and it drives Feltmoss growth. The Highgrowth is a biome built around capturing it.

**Sound:** Never fully quiet. Vellin communication (subsonic, low-register) is the constant background — audible as a faint rhythmic pressure rather than discrete sounds. Keth wingbeats are detectable above the canopy before the animals are visible. When the Highgrowth goes fully silent, something is wrong. The researcher notes this specifically when it happens.

**Smell:** Biological. Dense. The concentrated byproducts of fast metabolism and high turnover. Researcher noted it as "the smell of something working hard" in early journal entries. After many cycles it becomes invisible to them.

**Temperature:** Moderate throughout. Slightly cooler at the canopy floor due to shade. Noticeably warmer near the Highgrowth/Scorch Flats border where heat bleeds in from the Flats.

### Vertical structure

Three functional zones:

**Upper canopy / above-canopy** — where Keth operates. Open enough for aerial hunting. Vellin rarely come here voluntarily. Feltmoss covers every surface.

**Mid-canopy** — Vellin primary range. Dense lateral growth provides routes and cover. Skethran works this level as both a Highgrowth hunter and a crossing point toward the Understory border.

**Canopy floor** — dim, compressed. Vellin retreat here under Keth pressure. Skethran waits here. Mordath moves through here when in the Highgrowth.

### Time character

Fast. The Highgrowth operates at the highest metabolic rate of any biome. Populations surge and collapse in single-digit cycles. A Vellin oscillation runs 6 cycles and is visible as a dramatic wave. Keth follows Vellin with a 1-cycle lag — almost real-time response. Events here are frequent.

The researcher learns the Highgrowth first and calibrates their sense of "normal" to it. Later, the contrast with the Understory's pace becomes informative: the Highgrowth is not the baseline of the world, it's the fast end. Understanding this is part of understanding the full system.

### Residents

**Feltmoss** — the substrate the biome runs on. Covers every horizontal surface of the upper and mid-canopy. Its health determines the carrying capacity of everything above it in the food web. Cycles with the Vellin grazing pressure.

**Vellin** — the emotional center of the Highgrowth. Fast, social, canopy-dependent, audible before visible. They define what the Highgrowth sounds like.

**Keth** — aerial pressure from above. Experienced as weather more than individuals. When Keth density is high, the upper canopy has a particular quality — Vellin compressed, movement cautious, the biome holding its breath.

**Skethran** — primary hunter at the canopy floor, waiting below Vellin's mid-canopy range. Ranges the Highgrowth extensively but equally comfortable in the Understory (0.8 comfort). When Skethran concentration shifts from Highgrowth to Understory, something has changed in the prey balance.

**Mordath** — home biome, though it hunts primarily in the Understory and Scorch Flats. The researcher sees Mordath in the Highgrowth as a transit, not a hunting ground. Its arrival is still notable.

### What healthy looks like

Dense Feltmoss coverage across upper surfaces. Vellin groups of 4–8 audible and visible in the mid-canopy, grazing methodically. Keth present at moderate density — visible circling but not concentrated. Skethran occasional ground sightings. The biome is active, layered, and audible at all three vertical levels.

The researcher checks this baseline without thinking about it. When it matches, there is nothing to say. When it doesn't match, the deviation is the story.

### What stressed looks like

**Feltmoss crashing:** bare patches advancing across the canopy surface. The light-scatter pattern changes — more direct light reaching the mid-canopy and floor. Vellin will respond within 1–2 cycles.

**Vellin crashing:** Highgrowth goes quiet. The subsonic communication drops out. Keth pressure continues without enough prey; Keth will follow the crash within 1 cycle. Feltmoss begins unchecked spread — abundance that signals absence.

**Keth surge:** Vellin compressed and silent at the canopy floor. The upper canopy is active in a predatory way. The mid-canopy has a tension the researcher learns to read.

**Mordath present:** Skethran pulls back from the canopy floor. Vellin behavior is unchanged — Mordath does not hunt them and they do not appear to register it as a threat. The biome holds a different quality specifically in the lower zone: Skethran absent, the canopy floor unusually empty.

### Event character

High event frequency. Population surges, crashes, biome crossings (Vellin occasionally reaching the Highgrowth/Understory border), predator/prey lag events, Feltmoss abundance and scarcity notices. The Highgrowth generates most of the researcher's routine event reading.

Crisis events here tend to be fast: a Vellin crash can go from warning to emergency in two cycles. The researcher learns to respond quickly or not at all.

### Researcher arc

The researcher knows the Highgrowth first and best. It is the most readable biome and the most emotionally available one — Vellin is the relationship that defines the early game. Over time the researcher's familiarity becomes almost automatic: they can read the Highgrowth's state from a single observation. By late cycles they may find it less surprising than the Understory, and they have to consciously avoid taking it for granted.

### Distinctive character

Speed and legibility. The Highgrowth tells its story loudly and quickly. It is the biome where the researcher feels most like a naturalist who understands what they're looking at — and therefore the biome where being wrong is most instructive.

### Writing rules

- Active, present-tense energy in observation entries. Things are happening.
- Sound is a key signal — the researcher listens to the Highgrowth as much as they watch it.
- The vertical structure matters: upper canopy / mid-canopy / canopy floor are different places with different characters. Entries should locate the action vertically.
- Vellin defines the emotional temperature of any Highgrowth entry. When Vellin is well, the biome is well. When Vellin is silent, the researcher feels it before they measure it.
- Keth as weather: "Keth heavy today" as a condition, not a sighting.
- Never describe the Highgrowth as peaceful. It can be stable, but it is always working.

## UNDERSTORY

*Resident species: Nightroot, Woldren, Grubmere, Skethran (border 0.5), Mordath (comfort 0.8)*

### Physical environment

Below the Highgrowth. The base structures of the column-growths extend down into the Understory as wide buttress-root systems — massive, gnarled, forming the walls and corridors of the lower biome. The Understory floor is entirely covered in Nightroot: pale ivory-white root mass, flat and continuous, bright enough in the permanent near-dark to be navigable. The ceiling overhead is the underside of the Highgrowth canopy floor — a dark, irregular surface through which almost no direct light passes.

The space between is the Understory: wide corridors formed by buttress roots, occasional open floor areas where the root structures give way, and near-complete darkness broken only by the Nightroot glow-scatter from below and whatever incidental bioluminescence individual organisms carry. Woldren's pale flecks provide occasional moving points of dim light.

There is a weight to the Understory that the researcher notices immediately and stops noticing quickly. The air moves differently here. Everything sounds different. It is not the Highgrowth reduced in brightness — it is a fundamentally different place that happens to exist directly beneath it.

### Sensory character

**Light:** Dim, structural. The Nightroot floor provides a pale scatter that allows navigation without direct illumination. Objects at 10–15 meters are visible as shapes. Fine detail requires proximity. The researcher's field notes from the first Understory visit mention adjusting: "different kind of looking — more about shape and position than surface."

**Sound:** Slow and low. The dense root structures absorb and diffuse sound differently than the open Highgrowth. Woldren movement is a very low vibration more felt than heard — the researcher describes it as "the substrate conducting something large." Grubmere is entirely inaudible. The Understory is quieter than the Highgrowth but not in the way that signals danger — just in the way that signals a different pace.

**Smell:** Old, mineral, complex. The continuous Nightroot coverage produces a stable chemical signature the researcher noted on first entry and which never changes. Beneath it, something else — the Grubmere processing chemistry, probably, though the researcher can't identify it directly.

**Temperature:** Cooler than the Highgrowth, stable. No heat from above (blocked by the canopy), no heat from below (no geological activity here, unlike the Scorch Flats). The researcher finds the Understory temperature the most comfortable of the three biomes and stops noticing it.

### Vertical structure

Two functional zones:

**Root corridor level** — the maze of buttress-root walls and their immediate zones. Woldren move through here. Skethran hunts here. The researcher navigates here. Nightroot covers the floor of every corridor.

**Sub-surface** — the space beneath the Nightroot layer. Grubmere territory. Inaccessible to direct observation. The researcher infers what happens here from surface effects.

### Time character

Slow. The Understory operates at a fraction of the Highgrowth's metabolic pace. Woldren populations shift across 8-cycle arcs. Nightroot is near-static. Events here are infrequent but more significant per event than the Highgrowth — when something changes in the Understory, it has usually been building for a long time.

The researcher learns to read the Understory on its own terms, not by Highgrowth standards. "Nothing happened" in the Understory for 10 cycles is not absence of information — it is a state. Silence here is data.

### Residents

**Nightroot** — the floor. Continuous, ancient, white. Everything in the Understory navigates on or through it. Its health is foundational in the literal sense: the physical substrate of the biome.

**Woldren** — the large consumer, rarely encountered. Detectable by scrape-marks in the Nightroot, by low vibration, and occasionally by direct sighting. The researcher's understanding of Woldren is the most incomplete of any named species — which is significant given how many cycles they've spent in this biome.

**Grubmere** — invisible. Known only by effects. Everything that dies in the Understory passes through the Grubmere layer. The researcher tracks them through Nightroot recovery rates, floor conditions, dead matter accumulation.

**Skethran** — a visitor who belongs here. 0.8 comfort reflects genuine capability; Skethran hunts effectively in the Understory and the population here is significant. But it's not home. The researcher observes Skethran in the Understory as a different animal somehow — more patient, more ground-level, less of the fluid open-range movement it shows in the Highgrowth.

**Mordath** — hunts Woldren here. 0.8 comfort means it enters regularly and functions well. When Mordath is present in the Understory, the researcher knows by Woldren's absence before they see Mordath directly.

### What healthy looks like

Nightroot continuous and white across the full corridor floors. No visible accumulation of dead matter. Woldren scrape-marks fresh at several locations. Skethran visible occasionally. Grubmere inferred active from good floor processing. The Understory feels old but functional — the sense of something that has been running steadily for a very long time and intends to continue.

### What stressed looks like

**Nightroot declining:** floor coverage recedes at margins, pale surface becoming patchy. Woldren population must be elevated or something else is affecting root health. The floor feels wrong.

**Woldren crisis:** scrape-marks absent. Dead matter accumulating (Grubmere may surge in response). The Understory feels emptier, which is hard to articulate when it always feels sparse. The absence is qualitative.

**Grubmere crisis:** dead matter builds on the floor. The Nightroot layer looks different — more varied, less uniform. The chemical smell changes in a way the researcher might not consciously register for several cycles before noting it. The ecosystem debt accumulating.

**Mordath in residence:** Woldren withdraw deeper. Skethran reduces Understory time. The corridors have a different quality of stillness — the Highgrowth stillness is tense; the Understory stillness under Mordath pressure is something else, something older.

### Event character

Low frequency, high weight. A Woldren extinction warning is a major event — more significant per event than most Highgrowth crises because the Understory signals slowly and the researcher often misses the early stages. Grubmere events are almost always emergencies by the time they fire. Skethran biome-shift events (entering or leaving the Understory) are early warnings for prey pressure.

### Researcher arc

The Understory is the biome the researcher understands least for the longest time. It is too slow for their Highgrowth-calibrated expectations, too dark for confident observation, and its residents are the least visible in the ecosystem. By mid-game, the researcher has developed a different practice for the Understory: not active observation but careful inference. They read the floor. They note absence. They track indirect indicators. Late-game familiarity with the Understory is earned and it feels earned — the researcher knows this biome, but they know it the way you know a place you have had to learn slowly and partially.

### Distinctive character

Depth and age. The Understory feels as if it was here before everything else, and will be here after. Its slow pace is not inertia — it is a different time scale running underneath the Highgrowth's fast metabolism. The researcher's relationship with it changes their understanding of the whole ecosystem: what looks like stability in the Highgrowth is often just fast oscillation around a mean; the Understory's stability is actual.

### Writing rules

- Low register. Calm, slow sentences. Nothing urgent unless it is.
- The darkness is present but not threatening — it's the normal condition, not a hazard.
- Indirect observation throughout: scrape-marks, accumulation, absence, floor conditions.
- The sense of age in everything. Nightroot and the buttress structures are old beyond the researcher's ability to estimate.
- When things go wrong in the Understory, they go wrong slowly and then suddenly. The writing should reflect this: long calm building to a specific observation that changes everything.
- Woldren entries especially: questions outnumber answers. The researcher is always partially in the dark about this species — which is appropriate given the literal conditions.
- The Grubmere is never directly seen except in rare sightings. All other entries are inference.

## SCORCH FLATS

*Resident species: Scaleweed, Brack (home 70% / Highgrowth border 30%), Torrak, Mordath (comfort 0.4, occasional)*

### Physical environment

Open. Flat. Exposed. The Scorch Flats are the most alien of the three biomes and the only one the researcher can see across in its entirety from elevated vantage points. The surface is mineral crust — geological substrate that radiates heat from below, dry, pale where undisturbed and rust-brown where heat has concentrated. The heat comes from beneath, not above; the Scorch Flats are geologically active, or at least geologically residual, the heat retained from deep crust activity.

Scaleweed arrays occupy portions of the flat surface: hexagonal plate structures, grey-iron, low and geometric. At distance they are nearly indistinguishable from the crust itself — the researcher's first field note called them "geometric crust formation" and this confusion is appropriate. They are the only living covering of the flat surface. Everything between the Scaleweed arrays is bare mineral crust.

Brack and Torrak tracks are visible in the crust. Brack tracks are shallow and frequent; Torrak tracks are deep, straight, and rare. The track network is often the primary evidence of what has moved through the Flats recently.

### Sensory character

**Light:** Full exposure. No canopy, no shade. The light level here is higher than either of the other biomes and comes from directly overhead — reflected off the pale mineral surface, it can be intense enough that the researcher limits observation time per session in early cycles, before they acclimatize.

**Sound:** Almost nothing. The Scorch Flats are the quietest biome. Wind over the exposed surface occasionally, the creak of thermal expansion in the crust, the faint grinding sound of Torrak moving over Scaleweed if the researcher is close enough. The absence of biological sound is notable; the Highgrowth and Understory are both sonically present in ways the Flats are not.

**Smell:** Mineral and hot. The crust outgasses under peak heat cycles — something sulfurous that the researcher found nauseating in early visits and learned to work around. At cooler cycle phases the smell reduces to a dry mineral baseline.

**Temperature:** The defining characteristic. The surface is warm at all times and hot during peak heat cycles. The researcher cannot stay close to the crust surface during peak periods. Armored species experience this differently — Brack and Torrak's mass provides some thermal buffering; Scaleweed's plates are adapted to direct heat contact. Mordath's 0.4 comfort here suggests it can operate in the Flats but not indefinitely, and not during peak heat.

### Vertical structure

Essentially none. The Scorch Flats are a two-dimensional biome: surface and near-surface. There is no vertical complexity. Everything operates at crust level. This makes the Flats highly readable when things are present — nothing is hidden by canopy or depth — and very empty-feeling when populations are low.

### Time character

Irregular. The Scorch Flats don't run on a consistent metabolic clock the way the Highgrowth does. Scaleweed growth is slow but not as anchored to cycle rhythms as Nightroot or Feltmoss. Torrak events are infrequent and can be separated by 30+ cycles. The Flats can go long periods with nothing reportable followed by concentrated crisis activity.

The researcher learns that the Flats' apparent quiet is not stability in the Understory sense. It's simply low event density. A crash, when it comes, is dramatic.

### Residents

**Scaleweed** — the only energy source. The Flats without Scaleweed are a dead surface. Its hexagonal arrays are the visual definition of what a functional Scorch Flats looks like. The researcher monitors coverage patterns as a health indicator.

**Brack** — the Fringe species. 70% Scorch Flats, 30% Highgrowth border. Brack moves the Highgrowth/Scorch Flats border constantly, working the Fringe where Feltmoss is accessible. Its population tracks both producers simultaneously, which makes it the most reliable cross-biome health indicator the researcher has for this border.

**Torrak** — the specialist. Massive, slow, purely dependent on Scaleweed. Seen infrequently. Tracks are the primary evidence. When Torrak is present in numbers, the Scaleweed is healthy; when Torrak is absent, either Scaleweed has failed or the researcher hasn't found them yet.

**Mordath** — rare visitor. At 0.4 comfort, Mordath can enter and hunt but pays a cost. It visits the Flats to hunt Brack and Torrak when pressure in other biomes is low. A Mordath sighting on the Flats is unusual enough to note regardless of the researcher's familiarity level.

### What healthy looks like

Scaleweed arrays covering significant flat areas — not continuous (they never are) but present at regular intervals. Brack working the Fringe, traceable to the border zone. Torrak tracks visible across the flat. The minimal biodiversity of the Flats means "healthy" is a low bar: the right species present at viable populations.

The Scorch Flats have never been lush. Healthy is not abundance — it's function. The energy source present, the consumers operating, the processing minimal but sufficient.

### What stressed looks like

**Scaleweed crashing:** arrays shrinking, gaps appearing between plate clusters. The visual change is stark because there's nothing else to look at — the crust is suddenly more crust. Brack starts moving deeper into Highgrowth territory (the border is thin). Torrak populations will follow within a few cycles.

**Torrak crisis:** no tracks, or very old tracks. The researcher may not see a Torrak for 20+ cycles and realize they haven't been checking deliberately enough.

**Heat anomaly:** the researcher's tools register unusual crust temperature. This is the environmental sensor's primary use in the Flats — peak heat events that could destroy equipment or push species temporarily out of the biome.

**Mordath present:** Brack and Torrak populations will show pressure within 2–3 cycles. In the Flats where every individual matters at low population, a single Mordath visit can change the Torrak population materially.

### Event character

Low frequency, high drama when it fires. Most Scorch Flats events are crises or near-crises. Population surges (Scaleweed has the most surge events in the simulation, 1,781 across reference runs) often go unobserved — Scaleweed abundance is background. Torrak extinctions (14/20 runs) are the defining Scorch Flats narrative. Brack as a dual-system indicator fires cross-biome events.

### Researcher arc

The Scorch Flats are the last biome to feel accessible. The heat, the exposure, the silence, and the low diversity create a biome that resists the researcher's naturalist instincts — there is less to observe, it changes less, and the changes it does undergo are either invisible (Scaleweed growing slowly) or catastrophic (Torrak crash). The researcher develops a specific practice for the Flats: wide-view monitoring, track evidence, cross-referencing Brack behavior. They never feel entirely comfortable here, which is appropriate — comfort in the Flats would mean ignoring the baseline hostility.

By late game the researcher has probably watched Torrak go extinct and return. This shapes their relationship to the Flats: a biome that produces loss reliably, that recovers reliably, and that never quite becomes familiar.

### Distinctive character

Exposure and resilience. The Scorch Flats strip away everything that makes the other biomes readable — no sound, no shade, no vertical complexity, no ambient life. What remains is basic: can something survive here or not? The species that do survive here are unlike anything in the other biomes. Their resilience is not adaptable flexibility (like Skethran) — it is specialized endurance. They can do this one thing, in this one place, at great cost, and they do it over and over.

### Writing rules

- Spare. The Flats don't offer much to describe; descriptions should reflect that.
- The heat is always present. Temperature as a condition, not an event (unless it spikes).
- Track evidence as the primary observation method. The researcher reads the crust.
- Silence is notable only when compared to the Highgrowth. In the Flats, silence is normal.
- Scaleweed in abundance is unremarkable (background). Scaleweed in crisis is everything.
- Torrak events should carry the weight of watching something ancient and inevitable. Not dramatic in the Vellin sense — more geological.
- Mordath visits here are the most dramatic events the Flats generate. Note carefully.

## FRINGE ZONES

The Fringe is not a biome — it is a transitional condition that emerges at each border. The same species that generated their profiles in their home biome behave differently in the Fringe. Fringe entries have a different quality than pure-biome entries.

---

### HIGHGROWTH / UNDERSTORY FRINGE

*The richest border. Where light fades into dark.*
*Species active here: Vellin (occasional), Skethran (constant), Mordath (transit), Woldren (rare incursion)*

**Physical character**
Where the column-growth base structures begin and the full darkness of the Understory starts, but before either is dominant. The Feltmoss coverage drops off quickly — there is not enough light for it at the floor of the Highgrowth, so bare growth-structure surfaces are visible here in ways they're not in the mid-canopy. Nightroot extends upward into the Fringe at the root-buttress bases, meeting the lower Highgrowth. The transition is visible: pale Nightroot floor becoming shaded canopy floor becoming dark Understory corridor within a short horizontal distance.

**Character**
The most biologically active border. High crossing frequency because both biomes are tolerable enough for most Highgrowth residents (Vellin: 0.6 Understory comfort; Skethran: 0.8 Understory comfort) and Understory residents have at least some Highgrowth accessibility. The Fringe concentrates Skethran activity — it is the optimal position for a predator who hunts both biomes.

**Ecological function**
Where the two food webs touch. A Vellin group pushed to the canopy floor by Keth pressure may cross into the Fringe — and encounter Skethran waiting there. A Woldren at unusually high population may range into the Fringe, encountering Skethran before ever reaching open Highgrowth. The Fringe does not belong to either biome; it belongs to the crossings.

**Writing rules**
- The quality of light is the sensory key: enough to see, not enough to see well. In-between.
- Vellin in the Fringe is notable — they don't come here without pressure. Their presence here is a signal.
- Skethran here is not unusual — it's expected. The researcher stops commenting on it routinely and starts noting when Skethran *isn't* at the Fringe.
- The most frequent location for a first Skethran sighting at the Understory side.
- Crossings generate events. Each first crossing for any species is significant regardless of tier.

### HIGHGROWTH / SCORCH FLATS FRINGE

*The volatile border. Dense growth meeting extreme heat.*
*"The Char Line" (researcher-coined shorthand, emerges after sufficient Fringe events)*
*Species active here: Brack (primary range), Mordath (transit), Keth (occasional low overflight)*

**Physical character**
The most abrupt border in the ecosystem. The Highgrowth structures end sharply — not because of a gradual fade but because the heat begins. The base structures at the border zone show heat stress: the surface texture changes, coloring changes, Feltmoss coverage stops before the border itself because the substrate is too hot for it. There is a zone of 20–30 meters at the border where nothing grows and the ground is transitional crust — not full Scorch Flats mineral, but not Highgrowth floor either. This is where Brack spends most of its time.

The heat is physical here in a way it isn't even in the Flats proper: the Highgrowth structure retains and reflects it from the Scorch Flats side. Early morning is cooler; peak heat cycles make the border effectively impassable for unarmored species.

**Character**
Volatile, as described in the GDD. Most crisis events originate here because it is where the Scorch Flats' fragile producer base and the Highgrowth's more abundant food web are closest. Brack's dual-consumer strategy was evolved for exactly this border: Feltmoss accessible from one side, Scaleweed from the other.

The researcher develops a shorthand name for this border after enough events. "Char Line" is the example in the GDD. Whatever name the researcher uses, this border becomes personal — they have probably watched Brack pulled between two producer crashes here more than once.

**Ecological function**
The Char Line is an early warning system. When Feltmoss retreats from the Highgrowth border zone (Vellin absent, Felt overgrows, or Felt crashes), it affects Brack immediately. When Scaleweed retreats from the Flats side (Torrak pressure or heat anomaly), Brack is caught. The border concentrates Brack vulnerability in a way no other biome position does.

**Writing rules**
- The heat. Always present at this border. The researcher feels it; so do their observations.
- Brack is the defining species here. The Fringe is their home in a way neither full biome is.
- The shorthand name (Char Line or whatever the player coins) gives this Fringe a personal quality other borders don't have.
- Event entries from this Fringe should feel tense even during stable periods. It is a border under pressure.
- Keth occasionally overflies this zone but doesn't hunt here — nothing here is prey for them. Their presence is atmospheric.

### UNDERSTORY / SCORCH FLATS FRINGE

*The deep strange border. Rare crossings.*
*Species active here: Grubmere (edge of range, unconfirmed), Mordath (theoretical transit)*

**Physical character**
The least visited and least understood border. The Understory's root-corridor structure meets the geological crust of the Scorch Flats at a point where both biomes are already in their most extreme states: deep Understory (furthest from the Highgrowth, darkest, slowest) meeting the Scorch Flats edge (hot, mineral, exposed). Nothing lives here voluntarily. No species has the combination of adaptations required: dark-tolerance and heat-tolerance simultaneously.

The researcher has visited this border the fewest times. Notes from early visits focus on physical strangeness — the abruptness of the transition, the visual contrast between the pale Nightroot floor ending and the rust crust beginning. It doesn't look like two biomes meeting. It looks like two incompatible things placed next to each other.

**Character**
No established resident species. Grubmere's range theoretically extends to the Understory edge, but there is no dead matter to process here — nothing crosses to die. Mordath theoretically could transit through this border (it has access to all biomes), but the combination of deep Understory and Scorch Flats is the worst-case comfort scenario for it.

Crossings from this border are the rarest events in the ecosystem and, when they occur, the most significant. They should feel like encounters with something the ecological framework doesn't fully account for.

**Ecological function**
Effectively none in the current ecosystem. The deep strange border is the place where the ecosystem shows its limits. Things happen here only in unusual circumstances: Woldren crossing into the Scorch Flats edge during an extreme high-population cycle (unprecedented), or a future catalog species with unusual biome tolerance establishing here.

**Writing rules**
- Rarely visited. The researcher may go 50+ cycles without specific note of this border.
- When something happens here, it is notable precisely because nothing should be happening here.
- The tone should be slightly uncanny. Not threatening — just outside the established pattern.
- The researcher's language should reflect uncertainty: they do not have established categories for this border because it almost never generates events.
- Future expansion hook: this border is the most likely location for unexpected evolutionary events or new catalog introductions.
