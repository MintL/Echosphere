# Species Profiles — Authoring Source of Truth

Reference document for all 11 starting species. Use when writing observation pools, event text, and decision copy. Cross-check against `src/data/species.js` for population numbers and ecological coefficients.

**Taxonomy of this document:**
- **Physical form** — what the researcher sees (specific, alien, no earthly analogues)
- **Movement** — how it travels, rests, feeds
- **Social structure** — alone, grouped, territorial
- **Ecological relationships** — what it eats, what hunts it, how those pressures shape behavior
- **Population dynamics** — behavioral and ecological shifts at high/low population
- **Researcher arc** — emotional register from first sighting to modeled familiarity
- **Distinctive character** — the quality that makes it uniquely itself
- **Writing rules** — what to emphasize, what to avoid, key tensions

### FELTMOSS
*Highgrowth | Producer | Pop: 65–1,194, median ~672 | CV 51.5%*

**Physical form**
Dense pale-gold mat that colonizes every horizontal branch surface in the Highgrowth. Not quite moss — more like compressed mineral fibre that grows a few centimetres thick in good conditions. No visible structure at a distance; up close, the surface is slightly iridescent, a faint shimmer that cycles slowly, possibly photosynthetic pulsing. It doesn't move. When a section is heavily grazed, it shows as a dark scar where the substrate is exposed. Recovery is fast.

**Movement**
None. Grows and retreats. A Feltmoss crash is visible as advancing bare patches; a surge is dense coverage spreading across the mid-canopy and upper horizontal surfaces.

**Social structure**
Not applicable. A single continuous organism or a colony — the researcher isn't sure. The question doesn't come up often because Feltmoss is never the interesting thing.

**Ecological relationships**
Grazed by Vellin (primary) and Brack (opportunistic, border-scaled). Everything upstream of Feltmoss — Keth, Skethran, indirectly Mordath — depends on its health. Feltmoss crashes are always cascades in slow motion: the researcher sees the bare patches before they see the Vellin numbers drop.

**Population dynamics**
- *High (800+):* Coverage so dense it softens the mid-canopy visually. Vellin population will follow upward within 1–2 cycles. No event fires; the researcher may note abundance in passing.
- *Low (below 200):* Bare patches spreading. Vellin compression incoming. Below 100 the Highgrowth food web starts to buckle. The researcher recalibrates what "healthy" looks like.
- *Crash (below 100):* Impactful, rare. The researcher is surprised each time because Feltmoss is so stable it stops feeling like a variable.

**Researcher arc**
Feltmoss is background. In early cycles the researcher notes it as "dense coverage, baseline" and stops there. It takes a crash to bring it into focus — the researcher suddenly realizes they haven't checked it seriously in cycles. Late-tier relationship is practical respect: they've learned to monitor it steadily even when nothing seems wrong.

**Distinctive character**
The invisible foundation. It is noticed mainly through its absence. Even at crisis level the researcher writes about Feltmoss instrumentally ("the food web runs on this") not emotionally. It does not attract feeling the way prey species do — it represents the ground condition of everything.

**Writing rules**
- Keep entries brief and impersonal in early tiers. Feltmoss is backdrop.
- At crisis tier, shift register: the researcher realizing a foundation is cracking.
- Never romanticise it. It doesn't have drama of its own — only the drama it enables.
- The surge events (411 across reference runs) mostly don't fire an observation. Abundance is unremarkable. Scarcity is the story.

### NIGHTROOT
*Understory | Producer | Pop: 100–600+, median ~400 | CV 27.4% | Zero crises*

**Physical form**
Pale mineral-white root mass covering the Understory floor in continuous sheets. No above-ground structure — it is entirely subterranean and root-surface. The white is not quite white; more the colour of old ivory, slightly waxy. It has a faint chemical smell that the researcher noted early on and stopped noting because it never changes. In the permanent low light of the Understory, it provides the only bright surface — a faint glow-scatter that makes navigation easier.

**Movement**
Extremely slow growth and recession. Changes at the scale of centimetres per cycle. The boundary of the Nightroot territory only shifts when the Woldren population is heavily elevated, which is rare.

**Social structure**
Not applicable. Continental. The researcher's working assumption is that the entire Understory floor Nightroot is a single interconnected organism, though this is unconfirmed.

**Ecological relationships**
Consumed exclusively by Woldren, which grinds it from root mass surfaces. Because Woldren is rare and population-volatile, Nightroot almost never experiences meaningful grazing pressure. Zero population crises in reference runs. Its stability is partly inherent and partly a result of its consumer being in near-constant low-level crisis.

**Population dynamics**
- *High (500+):* Coverage extending up the column-growth buttress structures, above its normal floor-level range. Woldren should be examined; if low, Nightroot will grow unchecked. No event fires; the researcher may make a one-line note.
- *Normal (300–500):* Steady, expected. The researcher checks it and moves on.
- *Low (below 200):* Unusual. The researcher's instinct is to check Woldren — this is likely a Woldren surge, not a Nightroot problem. Below 150, the Understory starts to feel different — less light scatter, harder to navigate.

**Researcher arc**
The researcher probably checks on Nightroot once every 5–10 cycles as a health indicator. They note "stable as ever" and record nothing else. The emotional weight only appears if it dips: the researcher has become so accustomed to Nightroot's permanence that change feels like the planet itself shifting. Late-tier familiarity is almost geological — the researcher sees it the way they see geography, not ecology.

**Distinctive character**
Permanence. Ancient in a way nothing else in the ecosystem quite matches. The Understory feels, to the researcher, like Nightroot grew first and everything else moved in around it. Whether that's true is unknown and irrelevant. It gives the Understory a sense of great age that the Highgrowth doesn't have.

**Writing rules**
- Low word count, confident register. Nightroot doesn't need many words.
- "As expected" is appropriate language for the known tier.
- When it dips, write it as a geological event: something ancient, slightly wrong.
- Never animate it. It is not a creature. It is a surface.
- The zero crisis record is part of its character — when crises arrive (in late playthroughs or unusual runs) they should feel unprecedented.

### SCALEWEED
*Scorch Flats | Producer | Pop: 50–350, median ~200 | CV 24.8% | 8 crises, 100% recovery*

**Physical form**
Hexagonal armored plates, iron-grey tending toward rust-brown at the edges, each about the size of a palm. They grow in overlapping arrays, low to the crust, no more than 10cm tall. The surface is rough enough to shred anything without armor trying to graze it — only armored species can feed on Scaleweed without damage. At a distance across the flat, a dense Scaleweed patch looks like tiled geology. The researcher's first field note described it as "geometric crust formation" before realizing it was alive.

**Movement**
None. Grows by adding new plate segments at the margins. Retreats by plate death at overgrazed edges. The hexagonal pattern is always visible even in heavy populations — there are no "dense" Scaleweed mats, only large or small arrays of discrete plates.

**Social structure**
Not applicable. Colony-forming in some sense — new plates emerge adjacent to existing ones, not isolated. An expanding array is a healthy Scaleweed population. Gaps in the array indicate grazing pressure or heat anomaly.

**Ecological relationships**
Consumed by Brack (cross-biome, partial) and Torrak (primary specialist). The Scorch Flats food web runs entirely on Scaleweed — there is no other producer in that biome. A Scaleweed crash starves both consumers simultaneously. Because of this, crashes are dramatic even when Scaleweed recovers; the researcher knows what's coming for Torrak.

**Population dynamics**
- *High (280+):* Arrays extending beyond normal boundaries. Researcher notes the coverage and watches for consumer response.
- *Normal (150–280):* Baseline. The Scorch Flats are functioning.
- *Low (below 100):* Torrak and Brack under direct pressure. Below 75, the researcher is watching for Torrak extinction warning.
- *Crash (below 50):* The Scorch Flats are failing. Recovery will come — it always has — but the timeline is long.

**Researcher arc**
Early tier: the researcher is still surprised that something lives on the Scorch Flats at all. Scaleweed is the first sign that the Flats are an ecosystem and not a wasteland. Mid-tier: the researcher treats it instrumentally — it is food supply, monitored as such. Late tier: the 100% recovery record becomes a kind of faith. The researcher references past crashes when noting new ones: "it came back before, it will come back."

**Distinctive character**
Resilience without beauty. Scaleweed is not appealing — it looks like broken geology, survives conditions nothing else can tolerate, and keeps recovering from crashes that should finish it. The researcher has a grudging respect for it that never tips into fondness. It keeps coming back. That's enough.

**Writing rules**
- Alien feel. Nothing Earthly. No green, no soft surfaces, no lushness.
- The researcher notes the geometry — hexagonal pattern, tile array, plate margins.
- Crashes should carry weight (the Scorch Flats depend entirely on this), but recovery is expected. Not surprised, just watching.
- 1,781 surge events in reference runs: most of these fire without observation. Abundance in the Scorch is silent.
- Avoid "beauty" language entirely. The Scorch is harsh. Scaleweed belongs to it.

### VELLIN
*Highgrowth | Primary Consumer | Pop: 23–440, median ~77 | CV 93.9%*
*Eats: Feltmoss | Hunted by: Keth, Skethran*

**Physical form**
See `vellin.js` for the complete observation pool. Physical brief: pale coloring, small to medium size (smaller than a Woldren, larger than it looks at a distance), moves in tight bursts. Groups of 4–8 typical. The researcher heard them before seeing them — a low rhythmic subsonic signal, possibly communication.

**Researcher arc**
The emotional spine of the game. First prey species the researcher names, first crisis they feel personally. Late-tier entries are intimate. See `vellin.js`.

**Distinctive character**
Social, fast, canopy-dependent, and — crucially — communicative. The researcher senses there is more going on than they can measure. They are never fully comfortable with how much Vellin seems to know.

**Writing rules**
See `vellin.js` for the reference implementation. Vellin sets the emotional register for everything else.

### WOLDREN
*Understory | Primary Consumer | Pop: 1–240, median ~12 | CV 138.3% | 20/20 extinction warnings*
*Eats: Nightroot | Hunted by: Skethran, Mordath*

**Physical form**
Massive, low-slung, moves like slow water through the permanent dark. The researcher's first estimate was wrong — Woldren is much larger than initially apparent, because it keeps its body close to the floor and moves with a fluid compression that makes it look like less than it is. Hide is dark — dark grey, dark brown, non-reflective — with scattered pale bioluminescent flecks irregularly distributed. The flecks don't pulse visibly. Their function is unknown; possibly thermal, possibly vestigial display. The researcher has noted them in every sighting without establishing a hypothesis. Eyes, if present, have never been located clearly. Head differentiation is subtle.

**Movement**
Slow, deliberate, low to ground. Feeds by pressing against Nightroot root mass and grinding, which leaves characteristic long scrape-marks in the root surface — the most reliable evidence of Woldren presence. Rarely moves more than a few hundred meters in a cycle. The 8-cycle predator lag (vs 1-cycle for Vellin) reflects that Woldren does not respond quickly to external pressure — it moves and reacts on its own schedule.

**Social structure**
Solitary. Two Woldren in the same area is unusual but not actively avoided — they do not appear to compete. The population is so sparse in normal conditions that the question rarely arises. When researcher sees two in proximity it's late in a high-population cycle and feels notable.

**Ecological relationships**
Eats Nightroot (which is stable), so starvation is rarely the cause of Woldren decline. Primary pressure is predation: Skethran (main), with occasional Mordath involvement. Neither predator specializes on Woldren as primary prey, which means Woldren decline tends to follow predator range-expansion rather than targeted pressure. The 8-cycle lag means the researcher often doesn't notice the cause until long after.

**Population dynamics**
- *High (50+):* The researcher sees them more often. This feels wrong — high Woldren population means predators are low. Not reassuring.
- *Normal (10–30):* Sparse. Mostly inferred from scrape-marks. Researcher may cycle in passive observations.
- *Low (below 5):* Extinction warning imminent. The researcher may not realize how low it's gotten because they've never been able to count them reliably.
- *Crisis:* Population 1–2. The last known individual. The researcher doesn't know if there are more deep in the Understory. They never do.

**Researcher arc**
The researcher never feels like they understand Woldren. Every tier reveals new questions. Sighted: something large and strange in the dark. Known: behavioral basics, still more questions than answers. Understood: the long slow rhythms become clearer, but Woldren's apparent indifference to everything — including the researcher — remains unsettling. Modeled: quantified but still opaque. The researcher can predict Woldren population changes without understanding the creature at all. That gap never closes.

**Distinctive character**
Ancient and unknowable. The Understory moves on a different clock, and Woldren moves on a different clock than that. It is not dramatic or aggressive. It simply continues, on its own terms, regardless of everything happening around it — until suddenly it doesn't.

**Writing rules**
- Questions that don't get answered. The researcher models the population without modeling the animal.
- Extinction is quiet. The researcher realizes late. "I'm not sure when the decline started" is the right register.
- No reaction to threats in early tiers. It doesn't flee, doesn't display, doesn't acknowledge. This is unnerving.
- Long-lived suggests individuals the researcher may have observed across many cycles. Late-tier may reference specific individuals ("the one I've been calling the northern individual").
- Keep entries sparse. Woldren reveals itself reluctantly.

### BRACK
*Scorch Flats / Highgrowth Fringe | Primary Consumer | Pop: 5–75, median ~20 | CV 93.9% | 7 extinctions/20 runs*
*Eats: Scaleweed (primary), Feltmoss (border-scaled) | Hunted by: Mordath (opportunistically)*

**Physical form**
Heavily armored carapace, sand-colored with heat-darkened edges, low and wide. Built for abrasion — the surface texture suggests a life spent moving against rough terrain. Not large, but dense. The researcher's first comparison was to a river-smoothed stone that had decided to walk. Moves with deliberate economy: no wasted movement, no display behavior. Its armoring makes it effectively invulnerable to most threats except Mordath, which is large enough to simply end the efficiency calculation.

**Movement**
Ground-level, methodical. Works the Fringe in systematic sweeps, alternating between Scaleweed patches and Feltmoss border-zones without obvious preference. Moves deeper into whichever biome has better food availability that cycle. Not fast. Never hurried.

**Social structure**
Small loose groups, no apparent hierarchy or coordination. Group size tracks food: when food is scarce, the groups dissolve and individuals range further apart; when food is abundant, they end up in loose proximity by convergence, not by intent. They do not appear to communicate or coordinate.

**Ecological relationships**
Brack is a dual-consumer: Scaleweed and Feltmoss. Its crisis condition is a dual-producer crash — both going down simultaneously. Because it straddles two biomes, it carries the risk of both while fully belonging to neither. Mordath hunts it opportunistically; this is a secondary pressure that can tip a Brack population already stretched thin by food scarcity into crisis.

**Population dynamics**
- *High (50+):* Unusual. Requires favorable conditions in both biomes simultaneously. Researcher notes it as a rare window.
- *Normal (15–35):* Sparse, consistent. They function at low population.
- *Low (below 10):* The Fringe is thin. One producer crash away from extinction warning.
- *Crisis:* Dual food shortage plus Mordath pressure. At 3–5 individuals, the Fringe effectively empties.

**Researcher arc**
The researcher doesn't think about Brack much. It's unglamorous — no communication behavior, no social drama, no particular behavioral complexity. They respect it in passing the way you respect something built for endurance. Crisis tier is when the researcher discovers they've been taking Brack for granted. There's an adjustment: something that always seemed like it would survive has suddenly become something that might not.

**Distinctive character**
Endurance without drama. Brack has survived 7 extinctions across reference runs and pulled back from near-zero each time. The researcher isn't supposed to root for it — it doesn't invite that. But late in a run, when it's on the edge again, the researcher notices they've started checking on it more often than necessary.

**Writing rules**
- Practical register. The researcher notes it professionally.
- The Fringe is Brack's conceptual home even if it belongs to neither biome fully. Lean into "between worlds."
- Crisis tier should surface the researcher's realization that respect has become something closer to worry.
- Surge events come late (cycle 156+) — Brack recoveries are slow arcs, not fast bounces.
- Mordath's occasional interest in Brack is notable — an apex predator briefly paying attention to something unremarkable.

### TORRAK
*Scorch Flats | Primary Consumer | Pop: 1–80+, median ~15 | CV 149.1% | 14 extinctions/20 runs*
*Eats: Scaleweed | Hunted by: Mordath (occasionally)*

**Physical form**
The widest mobile creature in the ecosystem. Enormously plated — the plates are fused sections of the same material as Brack's armor but much larger, almost structural. Low-profile despite the mass. Moves very slowly; the researcher's initial observation was that it moved "like slow geology" and the description has never needed improving. Leaves deep straight tracks in the Scorch crust. Single individual tracks can be followed for significant distances. No two individuals have overlapping tracks in any observed period — the researcher has never determined if this is avoidance or simple low density.

**Movement**
Grazes Scaleweed by moving over it and pressing down — the plate arrays beneath its body crush and abrade the Scaleweed into consumable fragments. It does not need to reach down or manipulate. It simply moves slowly over the food and processes it by weight. This makes Torrak feeding invisible at a distance; the researcher initially thought it was stationary.

**Social structure**
Solitary. Never observed in pairs. Population is too low in most conditions to make encounter likely, but the researcher suspects active avoidance — there appears to be regular spacing between the track networks. Unconfirmed.

**Ecological relationships**
Eats only Scaleweed. When Scaleweed crashes, Torrak starves. Mordath rarely hunts it — the energy cost of taking a heavily armored Torrak is high — but will occasionally. More often, Torrak populations collapse through starvation while Mordath is busy elsewhere. 14 extinctions across 20 reference runs; the highest of any species.

**Population dynamics**
- *High (40+):* Researcher encounters them across wide areas. The Scorch Flats can feel busy, which is strange.
- *Normal (10–25):* Sparse. The researcher sees them rarely. Mostly tracks.
- *Low (below 5):* Last individuals. Scaleweed usually in trouble simultaneously.
- *Crash:* Goes fast when it goes. No middle ground. Populations of 50 can drop to 2 in three cycles if Scaleweed fails.

**Researcher arc**
The researcher develops a kind of quiet fatalism about Torrak that mirrors the species itself. Early tier: surprise at something so massive and slow. Known: behavioral basics, mostly unanswered. Understood: the extinction rhythm becomes familiar — the researcher has watched this before. Modeled: prediction is possible but pointless. The researcher can see the crash coming and has nothing to do about it. Late-tier entries carry a weight that isn't grief exactly — more like watching an inevitable outcome complete itself.

**Distinctive character**
The geological animal. Everything about Torrak — movement, scale, feeding behavior, social structure — operates at a pace that feels more mineral than biological. Its repeated extinctions and recoveries read less like population dynamics and more like geological cycles: erosion and restoration, on and on.

**Writing rules**
- Slow language. Long sentences. The researcher takes their time with Torrak.
- "Ancient" applies here more than anywhere except Nightroot. Torrak feels old.
- Fatalism builds through the tiers. By modeled tier, the researcher is not surprised by anything Torrak does.
- The track evidence is a key motif — the researcher often knows Torrak is present from the tracks before a sighting.
- Extinctions are not tragic the way Vellin losses are. They are quiet, expected, followed eventually by recovery.
- The "crash from 50 to 2 in three cycles" dynamic is dramatically different from gradual decline. When it goes, it goes fast.

### KETH
*Highgrowth | Secondary Consumer | Pop: 2–270, median ~51*
*Hunts: Vellin | Vellin lag: 1 cycle*

**Physical form**
Large wingspan — larger than expected for the canopy density, which requires tight maneuverability and suggests Keth hunts primarily in open sections of the upper Highgrowth. Dark underside makes them nearly invisible from below; the researcher often detects them by the sudden behavior change in Vellin before sighting the Keth directly. Pale eye-spots on the ventral surface — unknown function. Possibly disruptive coloration; possibly nothing. The eye-spots are the most distinctive visible feature and the researcher has spent time on them without reaching a conclusion. Stoops fast. The strike is sudden enough that the researcher has rarely observed a successful hunt directly.

**Movement**
Almost always in the upper canopy or higher. Ground sightings are rare and worth noting. Soars in overlapping circles over Vellin habitat. When Vellin population is high, multiple Keth will overlap territories without obvious conflict; when Vellin is low, they space out further and range more.

**Social structure**
Loosely territorial in normal conditions. Territory pressure relaxes during Vellin surges when there is abundant food for everyone. No apparent communication behavior. Social interactions, if they occur, happen at altitude and outside the researcher's direct observation.

**Ecological relationships**
Strict specialist on Vellin. No other prey. This is a vulnerability: when Vellin crashes, Keth crashes. The 1-cycle lag is almost mechanical — peak Keth pressure collapses Vellin, then Keth follows within a single cycle. The researcher, once they understand this, can predict Keth population from Vellin with high confidence.

**Population dynamics**
- *High (100+):* Vellin in full crisis or just past it. The canopy is quiet in a particular way when Keth is this abundant.
- *Normal (30–80):* Steady predation pressure. Vellin populations oscillating.
- *Low (below 20):* Vellin must be recovering after a hard crash. Keth will follow upward.
- *Crash:* Follows Vellin crash by 1 cycle. Both go down together.

**Researcher arc**
The researcher initially sees Keth as a threat to Vellin — an antagonist. The oscillation changes this. By mid-tier, the researcher understands that Keth is as dependent on Vellin as Vellin is endangered by Keth. Late tier: something like respect for the mechanical precision of the relationship. The researcher stops rooting for Vellin against Keth and starts observing the system.

**Distinctive character**
Atmospheric. The researcher experiences Keth less as individuals and more as a weather condition over the canopy. "Keth heavy today." "Keth absent." Individual Keth are hard to tell apart, rarely seen close, rarely doing anything other than circling and occasionally stooping. Their individuality gets less attention than their collective density and movement.

**Writing rules**
- Write Keth as pressure, not as individuals. "Keth heavy overhead" not "a Keth was hunting."
- The dark-underside detail matters: the researcher looks up and sees nothing, then Vellin scatters. The detection is indirect.
- Close sightings are worth more words than distant ones.
- The 1-cycle lag is the key mechanical relationship. The researcher should internalize it by known tier and reference it regularly by modeled tier.
- Surge events (394 across reference runs) often don't fire observations — abundance at a glance is silence.

### SKETHRAN
*Highgrowth / Understory | Secondary Consumer | Pop: varies widely | 563 surge events*
*Hunts: Vellin (Highgrowth, lag 1 cycle), Woldren (Understory, lag 8 cycles)*

**Physical form**
Long-bodied, four-limbed, fluid movement. Dusty amber coloring in the Highgrowth that becomes darker and less saturated in the Understory — the researcher initially thought these were two different species before tracking an individual across the biome border. Medium size, medium speed, nothing distinctive on first encounter. The researcher's early notes are almost apologetic: "nothing unusual." Skethran is the connecting tissue and its appearance reflects that — it needs to belong everywhere, so it marks itself for nowhere.

**Movement**
Efficient and patient. Moves at ground level or low branches in the Highgrowth, true ground level in the Understory. Does not avoid the border zone and crosses regularly without the hesitation most species show at biome boundaries. Hunts by waiting and following rather than active pursuit. Researcher has watched individual Skethran track prey for half a cycle before committing.

**Social structure**
Mostly solitary hunters with overlapping ranges. The overlap appears non-competitive — Skethran don't appear to defend territory against each other, even during prey abundance. Researcher has seen three in the same Highgrowth section without visible tension.

**Ecological relationships**
Most ecologically connected species in the ecosystem. Hunts both biome prey species (Vellin and Woldren), which gives it resilience no specialist has: when Vellin is scarce, it shifts to Woldren; when Woldren is scarce, it concentrates on Vellin. This also makes it a vector: Skethran range shifts can signal which prey population is under pressure before the researcher notices directly. The researcher learns to read Skethran biome distribution as an early warning system.

**Population dynamics**
- *High Highgrowth concentration:* Vellin under heavy pressure. Something is building.
- *High Understory concentration:* Woldren population under pressure, or Vellin is currently too scarce for efficient hunting.
- *Low overall:* Both prey populations crashed; Skethran following.
- *Stable distribution:* Healthy balance across both biomes.

**Researcher arc**
Early: "nothing unusual" — a competent medium predator. Known: the researcher starts to notice the biome shifts, starts to read them. Understood: Skethran becomes an indicator species. The researcher doesn't watch Skethran for Skethran's sake; they watch it to understand the state of everything else. Modeled: genuine respect. The researcher has more data on Skethran's movements than on almost any other species, because Skethran's movements encode information about every biome simultaneously.

**Distinctive character**
Connectivity. Skethran is the species that makes the ecosystem legible. Its behavior is a running commentary on the state of everything it touches. The researcher would struggle to model the system without it.

**Writing rules**
- Skethran entries often have a secondary species in them. "Skethran heavy in Highgrowth today — Vellin must be building." It's the context species.
- The biome distribution is the key indicator. Always note which biome Skethran is concentrated in.
- Patient, not aggressive. The researcher respects the patience.
- The 8-cycle Woldren lag vs 1-cycle Vellin lag is mechanically important — Skethran reads Woldren pressure slowly and commits slowly. This patience distinguishes it from Keth.
- By modeled tier, the researcher may use Skethran as a leading indicator before checking direct population numbers.

### MORDATH
*All biomes | Apex Predator | Pop: 2–41, median ~10 | 2.5 biome entries/run*
*Hunts: Woldren, Brack, Torrak*

**Physical form**
The largest mobile organism in the ecosystem. Dense and heavy — not proportionally tall, but the mass is apparent in every movement. Moves with complete unhurried deliberateness. The researcher's first field note: "something large. Not Keth. Different movement entirely." It took several cycles before the researcher was confident this was a new species and not the same individual relocated. No visible external sensory organs — no obvious eyes, no obvious ears, no visible olfactory structures. The researcher genuinely cannot determine how Mordath locates prey. This is the most persistent open question in their notes.

**Movement**
Constant but slow. Covers large distances across cycles without apparent urgency. Biome crossings are regular — Mordath moves between all three biomes with no apparent hesitation, a quality shared by nothing else in the ecosystem at its scale. It is capable of aerial movement (the researcher observes it crossing the upper Highgrowth zone on occasion) but is not primarily an aerial hunter — its prey are all ground-level species. Flight appears to be a transit mode, not a hunting mode. Its ground track pattern suggests directed movement rather than random ranging: it goes somewhere, then somewhere else. The destination logic has never been deciphered.

**Social structure**
Singular. The researcher has never observed two Mordath simultaneously. Population estimates range from 2 to 41 across reference runs; whether these represent one individual, a small population of solitary individuals, or something more complex is unknown. The researcher stops speculating after a while. There is Mordath. It arrives. It leaves.

**Ecological relationships**
Hunts Woldren, Brack, and Torrak — not the high-energy prey (Vellin, smaller consumers) but the slower, more accessible species. This is counterintuitive to the researcher: apex predator going after the slow species while ignoring the fast ones. The likely explanation is energy efficiency — heavily armored slow prey is worth more per kill than fast prey that requires full pursuit. One Mordath arrival restructures prey behavior across an entire biome.

**Population dynamics**
- *Present in biome:* Everything within range compresses, avoids, changes route. The researcher observes population behavior changes before seeing Mordath directly.
- *Absent:* The biome relaxes. Woldren, Brack, and Torrak expand their range.
- *High overall (20+):* Extremely unusual. Multiple individuals must be present. Research projects could be built around understanding this.
- *Near zero:* The apex is absent. Everything will expand accordingly.

**Researcher arc**
The researcher never habituates to Mordath. Every sighting carries weight, even modeled. Early tier: surprise and uncertainty. Known: behavioral basics, but the sensory-organ question remains open. Understood: the researcher starts to feel Mordath's approach before seeing it — they notice prey compression and know what it means. Modeled: full awareness of Mordath's ecological function, but the individual organism remains opaque. The researcher writes about Mordath more carefully than anything else, as if trying to do justice to something that doesn't reward casual observation.

**Distinctive character**
Singular significance. Every sighting matters. Mordath does not become routine. The researcher never stops paying attention to it. Its motivations remain alien even after many cycles of observation. It is the only species that the researcher consistently refers to without a plural — "Mordath was in the eastern Highgrowth" — regardless of population estimates.

**Writing rules**
- Each sighting gets words. This is not a background note.
- Economy of language. Mordath doesn't need flourishes — its presence is enough.
- The sensory-organ question should surface occasionally in later tiers. The researcher still doesn't know.
- Prey behavior as leading indicator: "The Woldren have pulled back from the southern Understory. Something is coming."
- Late-tier entries border on reverence without being sentimental. The researcher admires Mordath the way they admire something irreducibly itself.
- Avoid over-mystifying. Mordath is an apex predator, not a supernatural presence. Its strangeness is ecological, not metaphysical.

### GRUBMERE
*Understory | Decomposer | Pop: 8–273, median ~31 | CV 95.9% | 20/20 extinction warnings*

**Physical form**
Subsurface organism. The researcher's entire direct observation record is: three surface appearances over many cycles. What they saw: segmented and translucent, approximately the length of a human forearm, moving with a smooth wave-body motion through root substrate. The translucency was striking — the researcher could see the internal structure, though not identify it. It disappeared back into the root mass in under a minute each time. Everything else the researcher knows about Grubmere is inferred.

**Movement**
Subsurface. Travels through Understory root substrate in patterns that are inferred from processing effects — where Grubmere is active, dead matter breaks down at a measurable rate. Where it is absent, accumulation increases. This is the primary detection method. The track evidence for Torrak is visible; the evidence for Grubmere is invisible and statistical.

**Social structure**
Unknown. Population of 31 (median) across an Understory that contains hundreds of species interactions — either extremely dispersed individuals or clusters the researcher has never located. The three surface observations were all solitary. Whether Grubmere congregates at all is an open question.

**Ecological relationships**
Processes dead matter: dead Understory organisms pass through the Grubmere. Its population tracks total Understory mortality — high death rates mean more food, so Grubmere populations are actually healthiest during ecosystem stress. When things are dying, Grubmere thrives. When the ecosystem is stable and mortality is low, Grubmere population crashes. The researcher finds this inverted relationship counterintuitive at first. Later it becomes legible: they are the measure of how much the ecosystem costs.

**Population dynamics**
- *High (100+):* The Understory has been dying heavily. This is not a celebration. Something was lost.
- *Normal (20–50):* Background processing rate. The Understory is in equilibrium.
- *Low (below 20):* Extinction warning range. The Understory mortality rate has been very low — or Grubmere has been declining for other reasons the researcher cannot identify.
- *Crisis:* The Understory debt accumulates. Dead matter doesn't process. The ecosystem health starts degrading in ways that are hard to point to — "heavier," the researcher says, without being more specific.

**Researcher arc**
The researcher barely registers Grubmere for the first many cycles. The first extinction warning arrives as a surprise — a species they've been treating as background, suddenly critical. The realization that it has been near-extinction in every single reference run, repeatedly, without the researcher ever addressing it — that hits late. By modeled tier, the researcher checks on Grubmere regularly, indirectly, through ecosystem health indicators, never fully trusting direct population estimates because they were never possible.

**Distinctive character**
The invisible debt-holder. Everything that dies in the ecosystem eventually becomes Grubmere food and then becomes Understory substrate. The cycle runs through them invisibly. When they fail, the debt accumulates visibly — but the cause is invisible. The researcher never fully understands Grubmere; they understand Grubmere's effects.

**Writing rules**
- Almost entirely indirect. The researcher observes dead matter accumulation, Nightroot recovery rates, floor conditions — not Grubmere directly.
- The three direct sightings are each significant; they can be referenced in later tiers ("the third time I've seen one directly").
- The extinction warning should read as a discovery, not a status update. The researcher hasn't been watching this; they should have been.
- "Realising how much I've been taking them for granted" is exactly right for the understood tier.
- High populations mean ecosystem stress. Never write Grubmere abundance as positive without noting what produced it.
- The translucent appearance at surface sightings is the most distinctive physical detail. Use it sparingly.
